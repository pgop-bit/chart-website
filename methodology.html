Overview

GIS-Based Multi-Criteria Decision-Making

CHART implements a GIS-based Multi-Criteria Decision-Making (MCDM) framework originally developed across 1,615,275 km² of continental U.S. offshore waters. The framework integrates eight siting criteria across technical, environmental, and socio-technical dimensions, weighted using the Analytic Hierarchy Process (AHP), and projects suitability forward to 2050 using CMIP6 climate scenarios.

The central output is a Suitability Index (SI) ranging from 0 to 10, computed using Weighted Linear Combination (WLC). Sites are then classified into climate resilience zones based on performance across both future emissions scenarios.

The CHART tool generalizes this framework so that any analyst can assess any candidate site — not just pre-computed U.S. grid cells — by entering raw site parameters and customizing criterion weights for their regional context.
Siting Criteria

Eight Criteria, Three Dimensions

Criteria were selected to represent the full scope of considerations documented in offshore wind siting literature, covering technical feasibility, environmental sensitivity, and socio-technical constraints.

Wind Speed Technical m/s 26.6% Primary determinant of energy generation potential. Viable ≥ 7 m/s; optimal 8–12 m/s.
Bathymetry Technical meters depth 17.4% Determines turbine foundation type. Fixed-bottom viable 0–60m; floating 60–1000m.
Exclusion Zones Regulatory km from nearest 17.0% Distance from marine sanctuaries, military zones, submarine cables, and oil platforms.
Grid Proximity Technical km to substation 10.6% Transmission cost driver. HVAC preferred <100km; HVDC for longer distances.
Hurricane Risk Environmental 0–10 index 10.6% Historical hurricane exposure. Extreme winds can destroy 50% of turbines at vulnerable sites.
Port Proximity Socio-technical km to port 6.4% Construction and maintenance feasibility. Specialized vessels require adequate port capacity.
Marine Mammal Density Environmental 0–10 index 7.9% Density of noise-sensitive species including North Atlantic right whale and harbor porpoise.
Vessel Traffic Socio-technical 0–10 index 3.5% Commercial shipping and fishing vessel density. Raises navigational safety concerns.
Weight Derivation

Analytic Hierarchy Process

Criterion weights were derived using the Analytic Hierarchy Process (AHP), a structured pairwise comparison method that converts qualitative expert judgements into quantitative weights [6]. AHP is the most widely used weight derivation method in GIS-MCDM offshore wind studies.

The resulting weight set has a Consistency Ratio (CR) of 0.018, well within the accepted threshold of 0.10, confirming internal consistency of the pairwise comparisons.

CHART allows users to override these defaults using regional presets or fully custom weight sliders, enabling analysts to reflect local planning priorities — for example, assigning higher weight to hurricane risk for Gulf of Mexico sites, or higher bathymetry weight for Pacific deep-water contexts.

Climate Integration

CMIP6 Projection Scenarios

Two Shared Socioeconomic Pathway (SSP) scenarios are assessed at the 2050 horizon — aligned with the typical 25–30 year operational lifespan of offshore wind turbines and transmission infrastructure.

Only wind speed and hurricane risk are dynamically adjusted across scenarios. The remaining six criteria are either geologically stable over 30-year timeframes or lack robust forecasting frameworks [7].

Adjustments are applied as percentage multipliers to baseline values before re-standardization to the 0–10 scale. Wind speed projections follow Hintermaier & Lin (2024); hurricane intensity follows Knutson et al. (2020).

All raw input values are first standardized to a 0–10 scale using criterion-specific functions. For criteria where lower values indicate higher suitability (grid proximity, port proximity, hurricane risk, marine mammal density, vessel traffic), inverse standardization is applied so that higher scores consistently indicate better siting conditions across all criteria.

Standardization Functions

Wind speed is linearly scaled from the 4–13 m/s range to 0–10. Bathymetry scores 10.0 for depths ≤ 60m (fixed-bottom range), then decreases linearly to 0 at 1000m. Distance-based criteria use linear inverse scaling capped at maximum practical distances. Index-based criteria (hurricane risk, marine mammal density, vessel traffic) are directly inverted: score = 10 − index.

Resilience Classification

Three-Tier Zone Classification

Sites are classified based on minimum suitability performance across both future climate scenarios — ensuring that a site classified as high-resilience maintains viability even under the more challenging SSP5-8.5 high-emissions pathway.

High Resilience

Suitability ≥ 7.0 under both SSP2-4.5 and SSP5-8.5. Site maintains strong viability through 2050 under any plausible emissions trajectory.

Moderate Resilience

Suitability 5.0–7.0 under both scenarios. Site is viable but benefits from ongoing monitoring and adaptive management through project lifetime.

Low / Marginal

Suitability < 5.0 in at least one scenario. Long-term viability is at risk. Alternative sites should be prioritized where possible.

In the original U.S. continental study, 138,625 km² were classified as high-resilience — 14–23× the area required for the DOE's 30 GW by 2030 target, concentrated primarily in Atlantic waters (99.7% of all high-resilience area).

Validation

BOEM Lease Area Validation & Neural Network Accuracy

The framework was validated by comparing suitability scores for existing Bureau of Ocean Energy Management (BOEM) lease areas against non-lease offshore waters. BOEM leases — selected through independent regulatory review using similar but distinct criteria — scored 2.11 points higher than non-lease areas (7.18 vs 5.07, p<0.001).

This advantage increased slightly under climate scenarios (2.13 under SSP2-4.5; 2.16 under SSP5-8.5), demonstrating that current lease areas are inherently climate-resilient and that prioritizing strong fundamental resource characteristics inadvertently produces climate-resilient site selection.

A feedforward neural network (TensorFlow/Keras) with three hidden layers (64, 32, 16 neurons, ReLU activation) was trained to predict composite suitability scores from the eight standardized criteria. Models achieved R² values of 0.88 (SSP2-4.5) and 0.91 (SSP5-8.5), confirming the WLC framework's validity.

Feature importance analysis confirmed wind speed as the dominant predictor (52.2% average importance), with bathymetry second (28.8%). Together they account for 81% of predictive capacity, validating their high AHP weights.
